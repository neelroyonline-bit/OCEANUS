What Are Coral Reefs?

Coral reefs are large underwater structures composed of the skeletons of colonial marine invertebrates called coral. Each coral is referred to as a polyp. Coral polyps live on the calcium carbonate exoskeletons of their ancestors, adding their own exoskeleton to the existing coral structure. As the centuries pass, the coral reef gradually grows, one tiny exoskeleton at a time, until it becomes a massive feature of the marine environment.

Types of Coral Reefs

  1. Fringing reefs: These reefs grow very close to the shore on continental land masses or islands. They are the most common type of reef.
  2. Barrier reefs: These are also parallel to the coastline but are separated by a deeper, wider lagoon. The Great Barrier Reef in Australia is the largest and most famous barrier reef in the world.
  3. Atolls: These are rings of coral that create protected lagoons and are usually located in the middle of the sea. Atolls usually form when islands surrounded by fringing reefs sink into the sea or the sea level rises around them.

Importance of Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are essential for a number of reasons. They protect coastlines from storms and erosion, provide jobs for local communities, and offer opportunities for recreation. They are also a source of food and new medicines. Over half a billion people depend on reefs for food, income, and protection.

Threats to Coral Reefs

Unfortunately, coral reefs are facing threats from a variety of sources. These include:

  1. Climate change: The main cause of coral bleaching.
  2. Destructive fishing practices: Such as dynamite or cyanide fishing.
  3. Pollution: From land-based runoff and marine debris.
  4. Overfishing: Which can alter the food web of the reef ecosystem.
